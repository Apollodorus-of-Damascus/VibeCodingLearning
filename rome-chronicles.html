Myth & Founding
Romulus & The She-Wolf
Fratrem occidit urbemque condidit.
Suckled by a she-wolf on the banks of the Tiber, twin brothers Romulus and Remus quarrelled over the new city's sacred boundary. Romulus drew a furrow in the earth. Remus leapt over it in mockery. Romulus killed his own brother and named the city after himself. Rome was built on blood from its first breath.
Romulus Remus Tiber
AUC I

509 BC
Revolution
The Expulsion of Tarquinius
Libertas a tyranno vindicata est.
When Sextus Tarquinius violated the noblewoman Lucretia, she declared her dishonour to her father and husband — then took her own life with a dagger. Her kinsman Lucius Junius Brutus roused the people, drove out King Tarquinius Superbus, and founded the Republic. The word "Rex" — king — became a curse in Rome forever after.
Brutus Lucretia Tarquin
509 BC

218–202 BC
War
Hannibal at the Gates
Hannibal ad portas!
The Carthaginian general Hannibal Barca crossed the Alps with war elephants in winter — an act so audacious Rome could scarcely believe the reports. At Cannae in 216 BC, he annihilated eight Roman legions in a single afternoon — perhaps 70,000 dead. Yet Rome refused to negotiate, rebuilt, and ultimately prevailed. Scipio Africanus carried the war to Carthage itself.
Hannibal Scipio Cannae
216 BC

Political Upheaval
133–121 BC
Reform & Murder
The Gracchi Brothers
Populares contra optimates.
Tiberius Gracchus proposed redistributing the vast estates of the wealthy senatorial class to landless citizens. The Senate had him beaten to death in the Forum with broken chair legs. His younger brother Gaius took up the cause — and died likewise. Their murders cracked open a fault line that would swallow the Republic whole within a century.
Tiberius Gaius Senate
133 BC

The Die is Cast
49–44 BC
Civil War & Assassination
Caesar Crosses the Rubicon
Alea iacta est.
Ordered to disband his army, Julius Caesar instead led his Thirteenth Legion across the Rubicon river into Italy — an act of war against the Senate. He crushed Pompey, made himself dictator perpetuo, and was rewarded with twenty-three stab wounds in the Theatre of Pompey on the Ides of March, 44 BC. His death did not save the Republic. It ended it.
Caesar Brutus Pompey
44 BC

The Final Reckoning
AD 434–453
Invasion & Collapse
Attila & The Scourge of God
Flagellum Dei.
The Hunnic king Attila cut a burning path through Gaul and into Italy itself, razing Aquileia so completely that it vanished from the earth. Only the personal intervention of Pope Leo I — and a plague among Attila's own troops — turned him back. He died drunk on his wedding night in AD 453. Within twenty-three years, the Western Empire he had wounded was no more.
Attila Pope Leo I Aquileia
